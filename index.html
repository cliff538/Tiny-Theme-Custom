Kodak Mini 2 Retro Photo Printer

Jun 4, 2026

I’ve been harping lately about printing your photos because it’s just so much nicer than looking at them on a screen. Touch ‘em, hold ‘em, pass them out. People love a physical print. You’ll thank me. Really.

I do most of my journaling in Obsidian and post my daily pictures there, but I still use a paper journal. I put my stickers in there, and I’ve always been bothered by the fact that I can’t add photos, at least not very easily. Until now.

I saw a review for the Kodak Mini 2 Retro and thought, what the hell, let’s try it.

This little thing Rocks! I love it. It’s small, fairly cheap, and prints surprisingly well. You can see my test prints below. I tried to give it the full gamut, and the only colors that were off to me were the dragonfly bokeh.

You can print 5 photos at a time; it hooks via Bluetooth to your phone. It’s super simple, easy, software, and this thing is small enough you could take it with you. As you can see, you can have the print edge-to-edge or add a simple white border. I love the border look. I print everything these days with a nice white border.

Online in the U.S., it’s $99 and comes with 38 prints. Refills work out to be 0.37 cents each, which isn’t too bad. Have you looked at printing costs lately?

It’s a four-pass, dye-sublimation printing process. I’m just very impressed with what it can do. How will it hold up? I don’t know, but I’ll be testing it and printing a lot, and give you my long-term testing results later.

My plan is to carry it on work trips, pass out a few pictures, and, I guarantee you, it will make people smile. I’ll also use them to add to my paper journal. My only complaint is that there is no sticker backing on the prints. I believe that’s because of the multi-pass dye-sublimation process it uses. But you can just carry a small roll of double-stick tape. I know other small portable printers use different printing processes and have a sticker backing if that’s important to you.

I also had some nice nostalgic feelings from seeing the Kodak logo.

I printed 5 photos in a row, then started another 5; it had to pause after each photo to cool down while printing the next 5. Not surprising for something so small. It would be rare to print so many at once, but it can be done; it will just take slightly longer. Other than that, nothing to report for now. Again, I’m just so impressed with the colors that it can reproduce.

When printing, the photo goes in and out 4 times; each pass, it looks a little better. It reminded me of watching a Polaroid develop.

Finished reading: Go Computer Now! by Ben Zotto 📚

Delightful book about the start of the personal computer, and Sphere Corporation, arguably the first company to ship a personal computer, beating all others to the punch, but quickly failing and being forgotten in history until Ben Zotto finds a box of old computer parts on the sidewalk.

Such a wonderful time in computer history. The story about the life and death of Sphere and its brushes with greatness. Bill Gates, Woz, Radio Shack, Apple, IBM, and others are in this story. I love the early history and how the personal computer came to be. And the start of personal Software, small-business accounting, and word processors that people could only dream about began to take shape. These programs were shipped on punch tape or cassette tape until floppy disks came along.

This is the first book I’ve read on the early rise of the personal computer. I’ll be exploring other books about the time frame now. There are a lot of twists and turns as folks battle to be the first.

Some interesting tidbits are:

  • In the 1960’s, software programs could cost $30,000, and a word processing program in the 1970’s dropped to $5,000 with the rise of personal computers.

  • I started computing with floppies and knew about reel-to-reel tape, but did not realize that early programs came on cassette tapes. And boy, were there a lot of problems with those, not to mention getting them to work with your computer, you had to be a hobbiest electrical engineer.

  • 1975 brings the first software distributor. Mel Norell with Programma.

  • Just the story alone of the BASIC programming language is fascinating. Without that on your personal computer, you were extremely limited in what you could do with it.

The author Ben Zotto has a wonderful website and Sphere Emulator that you can use to see how primitive the early computers were. Remember, this thing was cutting-edge at the time. If you really want the full experience, you can purchase a module.

Denoise: LightRoom vs DxO PureRAW 5

Apr 28, 2026

A quick down-and-dirty comparison between LR and DxO denoise. There is a very clear winner here.

I was doing a fair amount of low-light shooting with my OM-1 Mark II, and the noise was just killing me during editing. I recently purchased DxO PureRAW 5 and haven’t really used it yet. I thought I’d do a quick comparison. Nothing scientific here, and I’m not even sure how much will show up in the screenshots below.

The OM-1 is a 20.4 MP Micro Four Thirds sensor and does not handle low light very well. So noise can be a real issue at times. I’ve always just used LR denoise, and it’s been good enough, but after seeing what DxO can do, I’ll be using DxO for all serious noise issues.

Here is LR denoise vs. out-of-the-camera ORF (raw): You can really see how noisy the OM-1 is in low light!

Here is DxO denoise vs. out of the camera ORF:

Finally, here is DxO vs. LR: I zoomed in to 200% for the best comparison.

In Summary, I hope you can see the large differences in the screenshots. The clear, hands-down winner is DxO. I would love for Adobe to improve the denoise in LR so I could skip this extra step, but here we are. Something to look for with denoise is preservation of detail; denoise tends to soften your picture, leaving you with less detail where you may want it. DxO also did a much better job in preserving details.

DxO also provided Optical Corrections: Lens Sharpness Optimization, Vignetting, Chromatic aberration, and Lens Distortion, but I did not take the time to analyze these.

I’m extremely impressed with DxO’s performance. On the downsides, it takes a while to process, and it’s an extra step in your workflow, but if you have a lot of noise, it’s worth the extra time. My average processing time per picture was 17-25 seconds.

I also learned that LR isn’t as bad as I thought. I’ll still use it for light noise issues, or when time is a factor, but for your good shots where you want maximum noise reduction and preservation of detail, DxO is worth every penny and your time.

Whether this is worth it to you depends on the camera you are using and the conditions you are shooting in. At the time of this blog post, DxO PureRAW 6 costs $139.99 as a one-time fee. If you just post your shots to social media in low-quality JPGs, you may not even need to denoise. But if noise is an issue for you and you’ve been unhappy with the software you are using, it’s worth your time to test-drive DxO.

Traversal by Maria Popova 📚

How do you get from Captain Cook’s 1769 voyage to document the transit of Venus to Mary Shelley’s parents, Mary Wollstonecraft and William Godwin, and their books Vindication and An Enquiry Concerning the Nature of Political Justice; to Margaret Fuller in America; to frog legs; to Aaron Burr as a destitute boarder; to the world’s first chemical equation–“grape must = carbonic acid + alcohol”; to the French Revolution and the Reign of Terror; to Jane Marcet; to Michael Faraday; Ralph Wedgwood, the inventor of the noctograph; to the catastrophic eruption of Mount Tambora and its devastating global weather effects; to a doctor who wrote the first vampire-themed book, then later took his own life after being unable to convince the public of his true authorship; to phrenology; to Walt Whitman and his lost true love; to Henry David Thoreau, Fanny Wright, Dorothy Crowfoot Hodgkin, Ruth Benedict, Margaret Mead, and Frederick Douglass?

Maria Popova, that’s how.

Maria tells a story drawn from the ages, across time and space, bringing lives together in stories of love, cruelty, and devotion. She traces the butterfly effects of historic and mundane world events that brought forth inventions and prose that have endured the centuries, and humans at their best and worst.

Because of societal norms, powerful love stories built on mutual respect and equality were almost unheard of at the time, but are celebrated in beautiful detail.

She tells the stories of history, but deeply intertwines the relationships beneath them, following a trail of connections across space and time.

I’m struck by the magnitude of what we have lost in our knowledge and our sensibilities, and by the limitations placed on women throughout history.

Over and over in the book, Maria highlights the ways women were not allowed, were taken from, removed, suppressed, discouraged, and ignored.

Where would we be if, throughout history, women’s ideas had carried the same force and power as men’s, instead of the tiny fraction they have been allowed? Men and religion have a great deal to answer for.

She takes you beyond the veneer, beyond the quick reputational one-liners of people you think you know, but don’t.

Mostly, I think Maria writes about love — the power of love. You can feel that she writes about it as something she feels deeply and understands on a profoundly human level.

Maria says:

We mistake so many things for love: loyalty, sympathy, pity, desire but somewhere in the marrow of our being, we know what love is and long for that house of belonging: the whole of who we are, or at least enough of who we are, at home in enough of the other. Enough may be love’s other name, life’s only vindication the hardest concept for the human animal to grasp.

Maria puts felt love into words better than anybody I have ever read.

Every page has pockets of beauty, and Maria understands the hearts of imperfect people. Humans are complex, capable of cruelty and self-centeredness, but also of love. Custom Oura Ring Data Board

Apr 18, 2026

Poking around, I found out that Oura ring has a robust API, so I wanted to see what Claude Code could do with it. It took an afternoon of fiddling, but it seems to be a robust custom data board with about 4 years of data, that updates itself 3 times a day to a SQLite database. It runs on my Mac Mini home server.

I can download my CSV data from the data board, and I also set it up to run a monthly report that makes an Apple Note for me and files it away. I had it run monthly reports for the past 4 years, and it’s an excellent way to digest sleep data.

The best part is a second page on the dashboard called the Correlation Explorer, where you can see how tag data affects your sleep. You do have to remember to add tags, late meals, massage, medication, sauna, vaccinations, etc., and you can start to see what is helping and hurting your rest. You can see the evidence on my board. On this note, my wife swears that a hot shower before bed helps you fall asleep faster. That’s the next test that will be tracked with tags. We shall see.

I know a lot of people don’t like A.I., but I’m still in awe of the things I can build with it. This is so far beyond my skill level. And the data analysis that I can now do is truly helping my sleep. The older I’ve gotten, the worse my sleep has been. I’ve increased my sleep by one hour per night just from the Oura ring data.

I like the Oura ring app, and they have their own web view that is really nice, but being able to customize what you want to see and how is pretty neat.

If you have any trouble with sleep, I highly recommend using the Oura ring for 6 months or so, using tags, and figuring out what helps and hurts your sleep.

For the curious, here are some technical details.

The Stack Snapshot:

  • Next.js 14 (App Router) + TypeScript + Tailwind on the frontend and API layer.
  • SQLite via better-sqlite3 – synchronous API, ~23MB database holding ~4.4 years of history.
  • Node.js v22, brew-pinned on the prod host.
  • PM2 managing the Next.js process on port 3001 (alongside a sibling “Status Board” app on 3000).
  • launchd for scheduled jobs (not cron – it’s macOS).
  • Tailscale for access; the dashboard has no public internet exposure.
  • About 2,800 lines across the key files (ingest, queries, schema, pages), 22 React components.

The Data Pipeline:

Pulls 16 endpoints from Oura’s v2 API on a 14-day incremental window per run. Deliberately skips heartrate and interbeat_interval – those would 10x the DB for no visible dashboard benefit, and can be backfilled later if HRV analysis ever happens.

  • Idempotent by design. Every row is an INSERT OR REPLACE on the Oura-assigned document ID (with UNIQUE(day) constraints on daily tables). Re-running ingest is a safe no-op; late-arriving recomputes overwrite stale scores.
  • Two-column storage pattern. Each table has explicit parsed columns for fast queries plus a raw TEXT NOT NULL column holding the entire API JSON. If I want to derive a new column later, I don’t need to re-pull the API – just run a query over the raw JSON. Full-fidelity audit log, zero lossy transformations.
  • Every run is logged to an ingest_runs table (endpoint, start/end dates, status, doc counts, errors). Makes it trivial to see “did the 8am run land last night’s sleep yet?” – which I used twice this week.
  • ~150 API requests, ~5 seconds per run – well under Oura’s 5000 req / 5 min limit, which leaves tons of room for backfill runs.

Scheduling:

launchd fires npm run ingest three times daily (8:00, 12:00, 16:00). The multiple fires exist because Oura’s detailed sleep endpoint publishes ~1–3h post-wake, so the 8am run catches the score, later runs catch the detail. A separate monthly launchd job fires on the 1st at 9:15am to produce a digest.

I love tweaking my two blogs, mostly with custom CSS, but I have expanded into the guts of Hugo (the blogging engine) to alter posts with additional data. I love learning, and I love understanding how things work and why.

Now, with ChatGPT or Claude, you can have assistance and a teacher with precisely what you want to do and learn. This is what A.I. is for, in my opinion. Anytime you don’t understand something or why, stop and ask Claude. It explains everything to you (hopefully correctly). This is not an exercise in Claude just doing all these things, unless you want that. Use Claude as a teacher.

  • Be as detailed as you can with questions, send screenshots, and if you don’t understand something, ask, then continue. Use the teacher that you are paying for.

Setting Up A Mac Mini Home Server

Dec 29, 2025

I’ve always wanted one, but I knew I didn’t need one. Money/Time spent on something that you really don’t need. TailScale and how much I travel were the reasons I finally justified the purchase and took the time to set it up. 

I purchased a low-end Mac mini, an HDMI dummy plug, and an external HD for Time Machine. Hardwire the Mac mini into your router. To start the headless operation, you need to plug in a screen, keyboard, and mouse initially. If you don’t have a spare screen lying around, you can use an iPad and the Orion App with a USB-C Capture Card. This is what I did, and it worked brilliantly. Set up sharing, enable screen sharing, and configure the power-saving options you want. I recommend turning off power saving and leaving it running all the time, as that’s its purpose. Turn on auto-login for the account you will use to manage it. If the mini restarts, you want it to log in automatically and start Plex and TailScale. You’re still protected, because you’ll set the screen saver to require the password immediately.

After that is set up, you can unplug your attachments and plug in the HDMI dummy plug so the mini thinks a screen is always attached. Head to your main computer, find the mini on your network, and screen share into it. You should never need to attach anything to it now. Keep things updated, and work on it using screen sharing from now on. 

Numerous blogs, YouTube videos, and LLMs offer guidance on home server settings, so I won’t go into more detail.

I deleted the apps I didn’t want on the mini, logged into my Apple account, turned off cloud sync for desktop items, and installed Plex Server and TailScale. Plugged in a 4TB external HD for Time Machine and set that up. 

I opened Plex, pointed it to my home NAS, and ensured it started automatically at login. TailScale setup can be a bit involved, and here is where I reached out to ChatGPT for some guidance: I wanted an Exit Node and Subnet for my home LAN. There is some command-line work here that was beyond my skill level. 

Now, when I’m traveling, which is every week, whether I have my iPad, Mac, or iPhone, I activate TailScale, turn on the Exit Node for my mini. It’s just like I’m sitting at home. I’m protected as well, or better, than any commercial VPN, and I have access to any computer, NAS, router, or external HD on my home wifi network. 

I can open Plex and watch live TV or media on my NAS. I was nothing short of frustrated with Plex Server running on my NAS. I had nothing but problems, and eventually just deleted it. But so far, Plex running on my mini has been rock-stable. I still prefer using Infuse for the front end to Plex. It just works. 

The one area I have been unable to make work is printing to my home printers from the road. In theory, you can do this. I can make the printer print pages, but they’re blank. There is a communication error in the printer setup somehow. I found my printers on the home network, renamed them, and attached them in a Finder window. Tried to print, but it doesn’t work. I went into Printer settings on my home computer and added the printers by their IP addresses, trying every available communication protocol, but that didn’t work either.  I can open a Note and print it, but it prints blank. I’ve tried every different setting, consulted ChatGPT, and no dice. I don’t see a reason ever to print anything at home from the road, but I wanted to know if I could make it work. 

Another advantage of the mini server is an additional place to store user documents on your home network. If you’re uncomfortable having a document you’re working on in the cloud, save it to your mini with the security of Time Machine backing everything up.  You could also set up a web server to run a website. The internet offers many options and ideas for your home server. 

It was a fun process; I really enjoyed setting it up and tweaking things. My primary purpose and justification are the TailScale benefits; as much as I travel, having the ability to browse the internet safely and access my home LAN is peace of mind. So far on the road, I’ve had zero issues with performance or speed, doing all my internet needs through my home wifi. 

To put a finer point on this Exit Node/Subnet feature, when you log into TailScale on your iPad, iPhone, Mac, any device, and then activate your home mini server Exit Node that is also setup to be a Subnet, your device thinks it’s on your home LAN, you can do a IP address lookup and you’ll show back at home. You have full access to everything on your home wifi. (Except maybe your printers) When you surf the internet, you are using your home wifi. Any website that cares where you are physically (I know some financial institutions won’t let you log in if you are out of the country) will think you are home. This can also come into play with some streaming services.  The Exit Node gives you access to your home internet. The Subnet provides access to your home LAN. You can set up an Exit Node with or without a Subnet. 

I’m no expert, and my terminology could be wrong in places; for that, I apologize. You networking experts will cringe at reading this (actually, no networking experts are reading my blog, ha!) Set up a home server and play with it; you’ll gain some good things. 

If you have any questions, please post them below, and I’ll do my best to answer them. As always, thanks for stopping by.

Let's Talk Email

Dec 23, 2025

I’ve never put much thought into email before. It wasn’t a super big deal to me; I had no professional needs with it, just a regular online-life email. I had no custom email addresses; I just used what Google and Apple gave me.

Until you read a few stories about people being locked out of their email accounts and losing everything.

This brings email to the forefront and makes you consider who your email provider is and whether you have a backup plan.

Most non-professionals end up with Apple Mail, Google Mail, Yahoo Mail, or a similar service. Remember AOL “You’ve Got Mail”. I’ve had them all over the years, including Hotmail. The last few years have been focused on Apple, Gmail, and Yahoo. You know the routine: an Apple address for business, a Gmail address for website accounts, and Yahoo for my spammy stuff - I don’t care if I lose this email.

That’s always been enough.

Recently, a lot of fuss was made on Mastodon about an individual who lost his Apple account after being locked out for using a bogus Apple gift card, according to the story.

This made me deep-dive into my reliance on big companies and who controls my data, especially email, email addresses, email Providers, backup plans, and best practices to avoid losing access to your email.

I’m not saying this is the best way, but it’s a lot better than what I had. It may help you as well. I think it’s pretty bulletproof.

What you want:

Your own email addresses with a custom domain: They are yours; you never have to change them. You can move them to any domain provider that suits you. Mine are at Hover. If Hover ever goes out of business, or things get flaky, I can move my domain names to another provider. The important part is that my email addresses will never have to change unless I want them to.

An email provider/service that you register your custom domain names to: This needs to be a service that has everything you need from a provider: cost, aliases, good apps, and a good look and feel. You can use your custom domains with Google or Apple, but why? You are risking some real hassles. There are excellent providers with minimal risk, such as FastMail, Proton, and others. This is where some good research will pay off. Review everything, find something that fits your needs and budget, then follow the Provider’s instructions to register your domain names with it. Remember, you can always move Providers later if you need to. At Proton, and now want to move to FastMail, just move your domains over to FastMail. In theory, you won’t lose any emails, as undeliverable emails will bounce for days, always trying to reach their destination. By the time your domains are registered with the new Provider, emails sent to and from you during this transition period will eventually reach their new home. At least that is what I’m told.

I went with Proton Mail. They have easy-to-follow instructions for setting up your domains, provide email aliases, offer good apps, and encrypt your email from start to finish.

An Alias Email Address Structure/System: The next step is to plan which email address to use for each location. You’ll have your primary email address, your favorite one. You want to keep this one mostly off the internet. You don’t want a ton of spam going to it after all these companies get hacked, and they have your email you used to sign up for all these services and accounts. In steps aliases. Most email services provide aliases for you to use. I went with a custom domain for my aliases, too; this way, I have complete control over how they look and can maintain a regimented structure. I’m using a service called SimpleLogin. I attached my custom domain to it, and now, I can make a unique address for each online account I ever sign up for. This way, when it’s eventually leaked online, and I start getting spam to it, I can change it. I only have to change one email address, and the spam stops. Suppose you use your primary email address for all your online accounts; you’re stuck. You will eventually get tons of spam, and the thought of going back and changing hundreds of email addresses will make your eyes cross, and you’ll say screw it.

My Email Plan in Action

I have three custom domains. One is for my personal email, one is for secondary/recovery emails in online accounts, and the third is my aliases email with SimpleLogin.

My business and personal email are with my main/favorite email address. Every other online account has a unique email address, an alias. This is where your email Provider will help, or you can use a custom service like SimpleLogin. I’ve been using SimpleLogin for years now, and it’s an excellent service. I’ve had zero issues with them. When that alias email gets compromised, and it will, I change that one email. Easy.

For example, I just signed up with The Atlantic magazine. It gets its own custom email: theatlantic@yourcustomdomain.com. I use the name of the account I’m signing up for; that way, I always know which account it is and which email has been compromised. Hit the easy button!

I now have 150+ email addresses out there on the internet; they all go to the same email Provider and open in the same Mac or iOS app—clean, simple, and pretty bulletproof.

Benefits

  • Everything can be moved.
  • Nobody can lock you out of your emails.
  • You’ll never have to change more than one email address when that one ends up on the dark web for sale, and hundreds of spam emails are inundating your inbox every day.
  • Every new account you sign up for gets its own email address. Your-new-account@yourcustomdomain.com or a structure that you can remember. You’ll never forget your username again.
  • An easy backup schedule that can be automated if needed.

Downsides

  • You’re not getting free email anymore. All this is going to cost you something every year. But, with that comes a lot of peace and a whole lot less spam.
  • Once you get your new email address, you will spend days, weeks, and maybe months changing all your emails from that Google address to your sporty new custom ones. But it’s worth it. It will be the last time you have to do this.

Let me know if you have any additional tips or if you see a vulnerability in my plan.

Cliff Notes: Dreams of Analog

Dec 11, 2025

The Battle of Analog vs Digital

Always on the edge. Always contemplating, planning, threatening to go analog.

Caution: unorganized thoughts below: 1

As a Gen X’er, I grew up in the analog era, and I retain both the right and the skill to return to it. I grew up without computers, cell phones, social media, or major newspapers. We had one local paper, some magazines, and plenty of books. In college, I got my first pager so I could find a pay phone, fish out some dimes and nickels, then beep a friend’s pager to call me back. We used all kinds of numeric codes for contact me, emergency, party tonight, what are you doing, etc.

For all the good technology brings, sometimes I’m not sure the good outweighs the bad. To a large degree, social media has a significant adverse effect on our mental health. With my current technology,2 which is pretty substantial (Synology, servers, cloud storage, Tailscale, VPNs, Subnets, Exit Nodes, Macs, iPads, iPhones, two blogs, etc), when it’s all working, it’s terrific; when it breaks down, it’s incredibly frustrating. I’ve certainly noticed a degradation in my attention span; I’ve been known to doom-scroll, and my wife has accused me of having ADHD—symptoms of our short attention digital world. One example is the shutdown of the Creative Live long-form educational website. They cite the lack of interest in long-form photography education.3-4

Let’s envision our personal world without the internet. How would this work?

My parents are almost completely analog. My dad is entirely; he does not use anything digital at all. My mom uses light email and surfing with an iPad. No computer. Very little use of the internet for business needs. My grandmother has no internet and uses no digital devices. It can still be done in the modern world.

How much digital could you remove from your life? Let’s focus on your personal life; you’re stuck with what you have at work.

Ebooks: I like them, I travel a lot, and I can carry hundreds of books with me at all times. I highlight, take notes, and everything syncs via Readwise, then into Obsidian. Could I drop all that and go back to paper books? Yes. I miss the feel and smell of paper. I could still highlight, write notes, etc. For a while, I would write out notes and highlights from the book I was reading on 3x5 cards, then either leave them in the book or file them for later use. I enjoy going back and reading my highlights and notes from old books. I do this every morning using Readwise, but I do have that backup analog plan.

Journaling/Writing: I use DayOne and Drafts right now. Could I go back to pen and paper, maybe a typewriter? Yes. Would it slow down your writing, absolutely, and perhaps that’s a good thing. I still journal weekly in a paper notebook. I love the feel of a pen on paper. I print out pictures and put stickers on the pages. I very much enjoy analog journaling and want to keep it alive.

All things financial: Banking, paying bills, etc. This would be very hard. Can you use the telephone to pay bills and stop into actual offices to drop off a check? Yes, you can. Depending on the size of the town you’re in, this could be easy or very hard. My grandmother and parents still drop off checks and pay credit card and cell phone bills via the phone. Would I want to do this? No. Not at all, but it could be done if needed.

Photography: This is a big part of my life, a daily hobby. I love my digital camera and Adobe Lightroom, Photoshop, and ON1 software. Could I go back to film? Maybe even a small dark room to develop your own pictures? Again, part of me says this would bring back a lot of joy and simplicity. In high school, I shot on film and developed my own pictures in the photo lab. I loved it. I loved the craft of film photography and the darkroom, watching those pictures slowly appear, floating in the solution. Could be done.

To-do lists: I use Things and Reminders. They work great. Could I go back to a little notebook I carry in my pocket? Yes. Yes, I could. I have many Field Notes Notebooks that would be great for this.5

Surfing/News/Blogs: This would be the most challenging part. Losing connections to people that you follow on their personal blogs and Mastodon. When I left FB, Instagram, LinkedIn, etc, it was hard to lose some connections, but over time it’s been just fine. I can do it. But maybe this would also strengthen your connections with your community, your neighbors. I haven’t been to a local hobby group in many years. Not having the online world would change that for sure.

Business Needs: This would be impossible in some areas. Applying for jobs, grants, business forms, Federal and State applications, you may not have a analog choice here. It would be off to the library to use a computer. I think more and more is headed in this direction.

In summary, we may be moving toward a world in which it will be almost impossible to live without internet technology. But we can always limit it, watch out for the harm it causes in our lives, and adapt. Gen X’ers have seen a life without the internet; it was a good life. Is my life richer with the internet? Yes, in so many ways it is. I have so many more connections/friends and get exposed to so many more ideas/beliefs/thoughts that make me a better human being. That’s my lens I view all of this through, if it’s not making me a better, smarter, kinder, calmer, more compassionate human, then it needs to go. I think the good outweighs the bad if you control it and watch for the areas that affect you negatively. Long Live a Good Internet.

Notes from the series "The Eightfold Path".

Sep 1, 2025

I’m a daily user of the meditation app Waking Up. I’m not a Buddhist, but I love many of their teachings. Along with daily meditation, this makes me a better person and gives me freedom from ruminating on my thoughts.

I listen to many talks from Joseph Goldstein and usually take the time to make notes. I started a new series within the App called The Eightfold Path, which is hosted by Dan Harris, Joseph Goldstein, and Sam Harris. The first talk was approximately two hours long, and I listened to it once through uninterrupted, then returned a second time with the purpose of taking notes.

I thought I’d post my notes from the first talk. Perhaps this will pique your interest enough to want to listen to the entire series, or maybe even try meditation. It has changed my life.

“The Eightfold Path”

Dan Harris, Joseph Goldstein, & Sam Harris on the Waking Up App.

Day 1 - The Right View

Someone asked the Buddha, Are there still enlightened beings in the world? As long as people are practicing the Eightfold path, there will be enlightened beings in the world.

Enlightened, awakened: The uprooting of greed, hatred, and delusion.

Assess your practice, are you suffering less or more?

Generosity, Love, and Wisdom are causes of greater happiness.

Dukkha:

The Pali word is usually translated as suffering, but Joseph likes the inevitability of unwanted experiences. And why the inevitability? because everything is unstable, everything is continually changing. Things are becoming otherwise.

The monkey trap:

Hunters will hollow out a coconut and make a small hole, just big enough for a monkey to put his empty hand through. Inside is a sweet, tempting treat. The monkey slides his hand through and grabs the treat, but is unable to remove his hand while holding the treat. The hunter is approaching, and the monkey is trapped. Unable to remove his hand. It is the rare monkey that can “let go” and remove its hand. Because of the force, the habit, the deeply conditioned habit of grasping to what is pleasant, holding on.

You get to the edge of your comfort/discomfort:

Level of discomfort, painful emotion, you get to the edge, the goal of practice is not to avoid that, it’s a benefit that the practice leads you to this place. You can learn how to relax into whatever that experience might be without holding onto it or pushing it away. You are seeing the impermanence of the discomfort, the emotion, so your comfort zone gets a little bigger. With more practice, over the years, you are enlarging your comfort zone, ultimately to the place where you have no edges. So, whatever arises, the mind is relating to it from a place of greater freedom.

Earth, Air, Fire, Water. Plasma, Gas, Liquid, Solid.

The Two Arrows:

The first arrow is the pain. The second arrow is all the mental suffering you might add to the physical pain. The Buddha points out that it’s the second arrow that we can work with. How we relate to it is up to us.

Letting Go:

It’s not the best phrase, but using the phrase “Letting Be” is much better. If something is painful, you’re not holding onto it; you don’t want it. Letting Be is a better description. Let it be, watch it change, watch it go away.

Preferences:

It’s not, not having preferences. It’s not being attached to the preference. You’ve been planning the buffet all day long, craving sweet buns, and you get to the buffet and they are out of sweet buns. Ideally, this would not cause a ripple in thought, nor cause suffering. To hold what is pleasant and unpleasant with equanimity.

Buddha would visit the sick:

I hope your pain is diminishing, but the patient would say No, it’s getting worse. The Buddha would say, Though your body is afflicted, may your mind remain unafflicted.

Mental Suffering:

Our thoughts on the past, present, and future bring the most suffering. The real promise of meditation is that, though the first arrow of physical pain is inevitable, the second arrow of mental suffering in all its forms, certainly your suffering of thoughts of the past, present, and future, is something that you can wake up from.

The Buddha said mental suffering is worse than physical suffering. The teachings of the Buddha are how to free ourselves from that mental suffering.

First Step of the Eightfold Path is Right View:

One expression of wrong view is: it’s the feeling or experience of, I’m happy, or I’m sad, I’m afraid, I’m bored, etc. This is the common way we interpret our experience. The wrong view aspect is the claiming of that mind state to be the self, to be I. Which is so deeply embedded into our conditioning. So the relief from that suffering is to be mindful enough, aware enough, of the emotions themselves as being impersonal.

One Tibetan teaching says, Thoughts and emotions wander through the mind like clouds in the sky, no roots, no home. What we can do is to root those emotions in the view of self, and that is what is problematic. Our practice of meditation is to see the emotions, the thoughts, for what they are: moving, always changing, passing by. Do not root them into the ground, into your mind as yours, as I.

Right View sets the direction of your path.

The first level of Right View is sometimes called Mundane Right View. It has to do with the right view for developing greater ease and happiness in our lives. How do we live wisely in a way that reduces suffering, creates more ease, aligns us, and puts us in harmony with the way things are?

The understanding that our actions have consequences. The Law of Karma. All of our actions will bear fruit at some point, depending on the motivation associated with the action.

When we act motivated by greed or anger, motivated by delusion, that is like planting the Karmic seed of some future unwanted experience. On the other hand, when we act motivated by generosity or love, by understanding or wisdom, that is planting the seed of some future desirable experiences.

When we act, it’s usually without thinking about where this act is coming from, what the Karmic consequences would be. Without taking the time to consider our motivations. Where is this act coming from, where is it leading, and do I want to go where it’s leading?

Which feels better, being happy and kind and loving, or being angry and upset? Which person do other people want to be around? It’s so simple and so powerful.

In the texts, it emphasizes the value of generosity, a behavior to cultivate, not just because it feels good in the moment but because it has very good Karmic consequences.

Make generosity a practice.

On the spiritual path, time is not a factor. We, especially in the West, want results now; we don’t want to wait. The spiritual path takes time, and time is not a factor if we are on the right path, going in the right direction.

Deep Dive into talking about belief in the deeper parts of Buddhism, like rebirth and Karma:

The willing suspension of disbelief. We can be attached to our disbeliefs as strongly as our beliefs.

Saying “I don’t know” is very different than saying “It’s not true”.

Be open to the possibility.

Sam references Pascal’s Wager in relation to belief in rebirth.

Pascal’s Wager:

Pascal argued that when it comes to belief in God, we face a kind of wager or bet:

  • If God exists and you believe → you gain infinite reward (eternal life).

  • If God exists and you don’t believe → you risk infinite loss (eternal punishment or loss of salvation).

  • If God does not exist and you believe → you lose little or nothing (maybe some worldly pleasures).

  • If God does not exist and you don’t believe → you gain little or nothing.

So, from a practical, risk–reward perspective, believing in God is the “better bet,” because the potential gains outweigh the potential losses.

Joseph had a teacher who said, “You don’t have to believe it to become enlightened; it’s true, but you don’t have to believe it.”

Dan’s summary of Mundane Right View:

Understanding that actions have results or consequences. 
Understanding that awakening is possible.
The root of suffering is craving. 
Joseph adds, selflessness or non-self.

We don’t invite our thoughts to come; you can, but most of your thoughts come uninvited.

One technique is to treat your thoughts that pop into your head as if they are coming from the person next to you. It can help you not identify with them.

As the thought is there, ask the question, What is a thought? Not what the thought is saying. But what the thought is as a phenomenon. You’ll find that it’s not much. You are taking the power away from the thought. You can get angry, or jealous, or have a very strong emotion over a little, small thought. Take that power away. Look at what that thought is. The empty nature of thought.

Impermanence, Dukkha, and Selflessness.

Mundane Right View: Day-to-day right view, conditional, conventional, what are the actual ways of producing ordinary states of happiness. Super Mundane Right View: Transcending all the Mundane Right View and waking up

In Sam’s view, the most astounding quality of the mind:

Unexamined thoughts are everything, and examined thoughts are nothing.

There is almost no tradition in the West of philosophy or psychology that even acknowledges that fact.

We are completely in our stories.

Three stages of Tibetan self liberation:

  1. Writing on water: as soon as you write it, it vanishes.
  2. The knotted snake unties itself.
  3. Thieves entering an empty house.

I want to say a few words about the inspiration part. 

At first, looking at other people’s photography was inspiring. But over time, it started to have the opposite effect. I wasn’t learning–I was comparing. And comparison is a trap.

You can take in so much of other people’s photography that it can make you feel bad about yours. It can make you put your camera down with a sigh; why even bother? I suck; there are 10,000 better photographers out there sharing amazing content, and nobody needs to see my crap pictures…. and on and on and on—the dark side. 

A lot of this is from social media itself. 

Some from looking at/consuming more photography than you are out shooting. More Consuming, Less Making.

I struggled with this a few years ago and completely put my camera down and stopped taking pictures. I felt like, why bother, my stuff is crap. I spent too much time looking at other people’s photography, taking classes, and worrying about my gear instead of just being outside taking pictures, loving my hobby, and working on my craft. 

Be inspired, look at other people’s work, but know when to stop. Know when to get outside and shoot. Work on your stuff, find your happy place, and find your niche in this big photography world. There are limitless photos uploaded every day. In 2024, it’s estimated that 14 BILLION photos were uploaded to social media EVERY DAY. You can’t compete with that; you can’t even look at 1% of those photos in your lifetime. 

Find your group, find your niche, or don’t. Just take pictures if it brings you joy. Upload them to your socials, or don’t. Make a website, or don’t. Do what is right for you. There is no good and bad. There is only art. Your art. Do what brings some joy to your heart. Make yourself smile. And if you do upload your photos, I can guarantee that somebody sees them, and it makes them smile, makes them think, brings them a little joy, and most of all, inspires them to go out and take some pictures.

Completely.

Bringing a Little Joy Into My Day and Weekly Planning

Feb 18, 2025

There are endless blog posts about the latest to-do apps and systems. My goal is not to proclaim from the rooftops that I’ve found the secret to daily planning success but to share some thoughts on a few options that I have found very rewarding.

I have a constant struggle with Analog vs Digital; it perturbs me how much I think about this. I’ve given up worrying about which type of system it is, and I do what feels right and what keeps me on track. And, if that system brings a little joy to the process, even better.

I’ve been a Things app user for years now and love it. It’s quick and easy on all my devices and syncs well. It’s always on my desktop at home as a gentle reminder to keep me on track for the day. My problem with it is planning my week or my day. Things is an ever-growing constant list that mostly sits stagnant, and I have little interaction with it on a daily basis. I’ve tried building my day in calendar apps, and that works, but they don’t stay open. I close them at some point, and the day aimlessly drifts off down rabbit holes. One requirement I’ve found is that the day planner must always be visible.

When planning my week and day, I was missing something and wasn’t staying on track throughout the day. It’s all there in Things, but I don’t have any draw to it; I can’t touch it or grab it and alter it. I’m primarily digital, but I need/crave some pen and paper touch—not too much, though; I’ve tried paper journaling, and that’s too much. I love journaling in DayOne.

Enter Ugmonk. First, this is not a cheap system upfront, but by the time you read this, they will have a much more affordable metal version for sale (launching February 19th, 2025), which has been much needed. Also, this system is not complex; you could easily make your own with some woodworking skills or a 3D printer and 3x5 cards. The cost for me is worth it; it brings me a little joy, it looks great sitting on the desk, and I’ve stayed on track better throughout the day.

I’ve been using the system for over a month and love it. I’m still not sold on using the weekly and daily views together, but for now, it’s both. It comes down to how your brain works and what you need to see. I’m a person with an odd job. I’m away from home for 3 to 4 days a week. So, when I’m home, I need to see what is left of my week and what I want to accomplish that day. I never know what day of the week or day of the month it is; it just doesn’t matter to me. I have large calendar widgets on all my devices to help me remember. All I want to know, what I need to know, is how many days off I have until I go back to work and what I want to get done on those days.

I use the weekly card for this. On my first day off, I grab a fresh card and my favorite pen and map out my week in broad strokes; it sits on my desk, so I’m constantly reminded. Then, each day, I formulate a plan with the daily card.

There is something special about grabbing that thick, beautiful paper and writing on it with your favorite pen. It gives substance to the plan; it sinks it just a little bit deeper into memory by writing it out. It makes me smile; it brings just a little joy, and I like that. Plus, I always have paper to quickly grab to make quick notes using the backside of the weekly or daily card. When done with the day or the week, I can store them away, or I’ve been using them for bookmarks.

As an option to this analog version, I just learned about Tweek. I’ve used this alongside my Ugmonk/Things system just for testing. I think Tweek is really cool; I wish I had come up with it. If I ever want to drop Ugmonk, I’d go with Tweek. They have full function under their free plan, so you can test it to see if it fits into your world. But it’s sterile for me; I need some analog, I need to touch some nice paper, and I need to use my favorite pen. I need that little piece of joy to start my day, the constant visual reminder, and that satisfaction of marking off an item with my Sharpie.

I hope some of this helps you with your task list. Thank you, as always, for visiting my little piece of the web. Cheers!

What’s unique about the number of books read?

Why do we track how many books we read? Why not count articles, magazines, or blog posts? Imagine an app that monitors every word you read and provides a total at the end of the year. Perhaps the next wave of AI-powered glasses will track not only how much we read but what we retain.

But does the number really matter? If you quizzed me on all 30 books I read last year, how much could I recall? Would I even want to know my exact retention rate? Maybe I should focus less on quantity and more on comprehension. Could tools like Readwise, which helps revisit past highlights, improve retention?

One approach I’ve been refining is writing about each book after finishing it. I review my highlights, jot down my thoughts, and create a mini book report. Readwise syncs with Obsidian, pulling in my highlights so I can reflect on key insights. I structure my notes with sections like Key Points, Personal Impact, and Action Items, and I’m considering adding details on who might benefit from reading the book, whether I’ve shared it with others, and if I’ve blogged about it.

The Power of Books

Books offer something unique–deep knowledge, immersion into another world, and a window into someone else’s perspective. Yet, attention spans seem to be shrinking. The internet has rewired how we consume information, making it harder to sit down and focus on a book. Some people struggle to finish long-form articles, let alone entire books. Does that matter? Are short-form videos and bite-sized text replacing deep study? The answer seems obvious.

Reading is a skill that requires practice. We should guard against losing it. Slow down. Put your phone on Do Not Disturb. Get lost in a good story. Let a book transport you beyond the present moment.

I struggle with this too. Even after reading over 30 books last year, I sometimes find my mind wandering after just a few sentences. I’ll read a page, then realize I absorbed none of it. Even rereading it doesn’t always help. When that happens, I close my eyes and focus on my breath–just breathe, slow down, reset. Losing the ability to focus is unsettling, not just for individuals but for society, workplaces, and families.

A Modern Problem?

Is this inability to focus solely a product of the internet age? I’m not sure. I didn’t use computers until high school or have internet access until college, yet I remember struggling to concentrate even then. Maybe distraction isn’t new–maybe it’s just easier than ever to indulge in.

The real challenge is how we engage with information. Do we skim and forget, or do we slow down and absorb? Reading isn’t just about finishing books–it’s about understanding, applying, and growing. That’s what I want to improve.

I used to write/publish photo stories at Exposure.co. It’s a nice website, and it’s a great place to see other people’s work, but it locks your content and locks you into paying every year to have access to your content. With that being said, I don’t do the things that I do for views, clicks, or money.  I’ve been moving my digital life to cliff538.com and have downloaded my data for Instagram, Facebook, and Twitter. Today, I focused on Exposure. How much does it cost a year? Can I download my stories? Do I want this anymore? Keep or delete.  Over the years at Exposure I’ve been paying $99/year, the lowest tier now is $149/year. I decided to export my content and move it to cliff538.com. Well, guess what, you can’t.   3. Data Ownership: The data you’re generating—your audience insights, contact lists, and engagement metrics—belongs to the platform, not you. They profit from it while you’re left in the dark.  

  4. Censorship and Restrictions: Platforms can impose restrictions on what you publish, often limiting creativity and expression. If your content doesn’t align with their guidelines, it can be removed or suppressed.

Where Do You find Books To Read

Jan 6, 2025

This has been a struggle for me in the past. I often found myself running out of books and searching for something new to read, which led me to browse bookstores. Each visit is a wonderful experience; independent shops have a unique charm, often showcasing local authors, and the smell of books is irreplaceable. However, there were times when I left empty-handed, without anything that sparked my interest.

To tackle this, having a collection of online tools for discovering books is essential. Here are some ideas that may inspire you.

Now, I face a new challenge: a to-read list that keeps expanding, which is, in fact, a wonderful problem to have. I used to feel overwhelmed by this, but I’ve learned to embrace the reality that countless books will always remain out of reach. That’s why it’s so important to focus on reading what you love. Don’t linger on a book that feels just okay; there are countless extraordinary stories waiting for you. And don’t let that ever-growing pile of unread books weigh you down; it’s always there with your next book!

I will continue to add to this list with your suggestions and any helpful resources I come across. Please email or comment with your favorites, and I’ll include them.

Major Platforms

Five Books - They ask experts to recommend the five best books in their subject and explain their selection in an interview. This site has an archive of more than 1700 interviews and 8000 book recommendations. They publish at least two new interviews per week.

The Millions - A great online publication devoted to books. Check out The Great Fall 2024 Book Preview as a good place to start.

I haven’t tried any of their e-readers, as the iPhone/iPad apps are enough for me. I’ve heard really good things about them on Reddit.
